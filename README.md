# Momo Challenge

> ⚠️ **CRITICAL: THIS IS A HOAX - NOT A REAL PHENOMENON**
> 
> The Momo Challenge was an internet hoax and media-driven moral panic. Extensive investigations by law enforcement, journalists, and platforms found **ZERO evidence** of any actual challenge, coordinated attacks, or related harm. This entry serves as a case study in viral misinformation and media literacy.

## What Was Claimed

In 2018-2019, news outlets worldwide reported an alleged "suicide game" targeting children through WhatsApp and YouTube. The claims stated:

- A WhatsApp account with a disturbing profile image was contacting children
- The account sent progressively dangerous "challenges" 
- Tasks allegedly escalated to self-harm and suicide
- The profile image showed a bulging-eyed woman-bird creature
- Claims spread that "Momo" appeared in YouTube Kids videos

## What Actually Happened

**ZERO evidence of any actual challenge was ever found.**

### Official Investigations

- **YouTube**: "We've not received any recent evidence of videos showing or promoting the Momo Challenge on YouTube. Content of this kind would be in violation of our policies and removed immediately." (February 2019)

- **UK Safer Internet Centre**: "There have been reports about 'Momo' in the press and we are working with partners to find evidence of UK children being approached by anyone suggesting they take part in the 'challenge.' We are not currently aware of any verified evidence in the UK."

- **Law Enforcement** (multiple countries): No verified reports or evidence found

- **The Atlantic Investigation** (March 2019): "I could not find a single confirmed case of a child being harmed."

### The Image Source

The disturbing image was from a sculpture called **"Mother Bird"** created by Japanese artist Keisuke Aisawa for the horror art company Link Factory in 2016. The sculpture:

- Was displayed at a Tokyo gallery
- Had nothing to do with any "challenge"
- Was destroyed by the artist in 2019 due to unwanted attention
- Had been circulating on horror/creepypasta sites before the hoax

## How the Hoax Spread

### Timeline of a Moral Panic

| Date | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| **2016** | Mother Bird sculpture created | Art piece for horror exhibition |
| **2018 July** | First media reports | Latin American outlets report alleged challenge |
| **2018 Aug** | First investigations | Police find no evidence |
| **2019 Feb 25** | YouTube panic begins | Claims of Momo in kids' videos |
| **2019 Feb 27** | Global media frenzy | Major outlets run warning stories |
| **2019 Feb 28** | YouTube statement | Platform finds zero evidence |
| **2019 Mar 1** | Police statements | Multiple departments confirm no cases |
| **2019 Mar 5** | Major debunking | Atlantic, BBC, Vox publish investigations |

### The Amplification Cycle

```
1. Viral social media posts (no evidence)
   ↓
2. News outlets report "warnings" (no verification)
   ↓
3. Schools and police issue alerts (based on media)
   ↓
4. Parents panic and share warnings
   ↓
5. More media coverage (clicks = revenue)
   ↓
6. Searches spike (Streisand Effect)
   ↓
7. Cycle repeats globally
```

### Why It Spread

1. **Parental Anxiety**: Legitimate concerns about online safety made parents receptive
2. **Disturbing Image**: The sculpture image was memorable and shareable
3. **Platform Unfamiliarity**: Parents couldn't verify claims about WhatsApp/YouTube
4. **Precedent**: Earlier "Blue Whale Challenge" panic created template
5. **Media Competition**: Outlets competed for clicks on sensational story
6. **Lack of Verification**: Few journalists checked if challenge actually existed

## The Damage Done

### Harm Caused by the Hoax

- **Wasted Resources**: Law enforcement investigated non-existent threat
- **Increased Anxiety**: Parents and children frightened without cause
- **Artist Harmed**: Keisuke Aisawa received harassment; destroyed sculpture
- **Media Trust Eroded**: Uncritical reporting damaged credibility
- **Streisand Effect**: Warnings likely exposed more children to disturbing image
- **Real Issues Ignored**: Focus on hoax distracted from actual online safety concerns

### Potential Copycat Effect

Ironically, widespread media coverage of a non-existent challenge may have:
- Inspired some individuals to create copycat content
- Given troubled individuals ideas they wouldn't have had otherwise
- Created the very threat media claimed to be warning against

## Media Literacy Lessons

### Red Flags That Should Have Stopped This

✅ **No Verified Cases**: Despite "widespread" threat, no one could find actual instances

✅ **No Platform Evidence**: WhatsApp/YouTube found zero content

✅ **No Police Reports**: Law enforcement had no documented victims

✅ **Anonymous Sources**: Stories relied on vague "reports" and "warnings"

✅ **Moral Panic Pattern**: Classic "threat to children" fear-based narrative

✅ **Viral Image Without Context**: Disturbing picture spread separately from any evidence

### How to Avoid Falling for Hoaxes

1. **Verify Before Sharing**: Check multiple credible sources
2. **Question Sensational Claims**: Extraordinary claims need extraordinary evidence
3. **Check Fact-Checking Sites**: Snopes, BBC Reality Check, PolitiFact
4. **Consider the Source**: Distinguish news from viral social media posts
5. **Look for Primary Sources**: Did anyone actually document the threat?
6. **Beware "Threats to Children"**: Moral panics often target parental fears
7. **Check Official Statements**: What do platforms and police actually say?

- **Verification Failure**: Journalism prioritizing speed over accuracy

## Journalism Failures

### What Went Wrong

Major news outlets failed basic journalistic standards:

❌ **No Verification**: Reported "warnings" without confirming challenge existed

❌ **Anonymous Sources**: Relied on vague "reports" and secondhand claims

❌ **Headline Sensationalism**: "Deadly Momo Challenge" despite zero deaths

❌ **Platform Amplification**: Spread awareness of hoax to wider audience

❌ **Minimal Corrections**: Debunking received fraction of initial coverage

### Better Practices

✅ Independently verify claims before publication

✅ Distinguish between "people are talking about" and "this is real"

✅ Consider whether coverage might cause harm (Streisand Effect)

✅ Give corrections equal prominence to original stories

✅ Educate rather than sensationalize
